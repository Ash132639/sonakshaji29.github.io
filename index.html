Seminar Task 1 · SWOT Analysis

Canal+: Navigating the Streaming Wars

Canal+ is a French pay-TV giant facing an existential question: how do you compete when Netflix, Disney+ and Amazon Prime are all gunning for the same living room?

The streaming landscape — increasingly crowded, increasingly global.

Canal+ has been a dominant force in European pay-TV since the 1980s, but the rise of global streaming giants has fundamentally altered the competitive landscape. Using SWOT analysis, we can map out where Canal+ genuinely leads — and where it's exposed.

The SWOT Breakdown

💪 Strengths

  • Strong brand & market reputation
  • Premium content & exclusive rights
  • Diverse portfolio — TV, streaming, cinema
  • Strong presence in France & Africa
  • Integrated production operations

⚠️ Weaknesses

  • High content acquisition costs
  • Dependence on traditional markets
  • Complex, expensive cost structure
  • Vulnerable to global streamer pressure

🚀 Opportunities

  • Growth in streaming & digital services
  • Expansion in emerging markets
  • Partnerships & co-productions
  • Personalisation & data analytics
  • Sports broadcasting rights

🔥 Threats

  • Netflix, Disney+, Amazon Prime pressure
  • Piracy & shifting consumer habits
  • Economic downturns affecting spend
  • Regulatory & licensing risks

My Take

What struck me most in the seminar is that Canal+'s Africa presence is massively underrated. While Netflix pours money into local content in the US and UK, Canal+ has had boots on the ground in Francophone Africa for years. That's a moat Netflix would take a decade to replicate.

The weakness, though, is real: Canal+ is stuck between two identities. It's too premium to win on pure price, but not big enough to outspend Netflix on content. The strategic answer has to be co-production and niche ownership — go deep in sports and local drama, not broad.

🤔 Seminar Reflection Questions

  1. How well is Canal+ leveraging its brand strength in digital channels — or is it still thinking like a broadcaster?
  2. Is the Africa expansion a genuine strategic pillar, or an afterthought that needs more investment?
  3. Can personalisation and data analytics become a real differentiator, or is this just table stakes now?
  4. How should Canal+ respond to the bundle strategies of Apple TV+, Disney+ and Amazon?

Amazon vs. Walmart: The VRIO Battle

Two giants. One from a garage in Seattle, one from a small town in Arkansas. Here's why neither has killed the other — and why the race is still on.

The battlefield: logistics, data, scale — and whoever cracks omnichannel first.

The VRIO framework asks four questions about every resource: does it create Value? Is it Rare? Is it hard to Imitate? Is the organisation set up to exploit it? Only resources that pass all four tests deliver sustained competitive advantage.

Their Distinctive Strengths Side-by-Side

amazon
Distinctive Advantages
  • Advanced technology & automation
  • Data-driven personalisation (AI/ML)
  • AWS cloud ecosystem & revenue
  • Fulfilled by Amazon logistics network
  • Prime membership loyalty flywheel
Walmart ★
Distinctive Advantages
  • 4,700+ physical stores as fulfilment hubs
  • Everyday low prices at massive scale
  • Omnichannel grocery dominance (US)
  • Supply chain efficiency & supplier power
  • Walmart+ membership growing fast

Applying VRIO

V
Value

Both create undeniable customer value — Amazon through convenience & choice, Walmart through price.

R
Rarity

AWS infrastructure and Walmart's store network at scale are genuinely rare — no one else has either.

I
Imitability

Both took decades and hundreds of billions to build. The barrier to imitation is enormous.

O
Organisation

Both are structured to exploit their assets — Amazon through speed, Walmart through operational discipline.

The Real Battleground

What the seminar made clear is that Amazon and Walmart are asymmetric competitors. Amazon's strength is digital-first and data-led; Walmart's is physical scale and price. Copying each other's core advantage would take years and cost billions.

The live race is omnichannel. Amazon is pushing hard into physical retail (Amazon Fresh, Whole Foods). Walmart is doubling down on e-commerce and same-day delivery using its store network as mini-fulfilment centres. Whoever cracks the seamless digital-physical experience first wins a structural advantage that's almost impossible to dislodge.

Key Insight

"Amazon can't easily build 4,700 physical stores. Walmart can't easily become a cloud computing powerhouse. Their advantages are structurally asymmetric — which is exactly why the rivalry is still live."

IKEA: The Art of the Hybrid Strategy

Most companies are told to pick one lane. IKEA built a business model where cheap and good reinforce each other — and then evolved it into a circular economy pioneer.

Functional, affordable, designed for life — IKEA's value proposition in one room.

Porter's generic strategies framework says: compete on cost, or compete on differentiation. Pick one, or you'll get stuck in the "middle" — mediocre at both. IKEA's 80-year history is a masterclass in why that rule has exceptions.

Four Strategic Pillars

01 · Cost Leadership

How IKEA Keeps Prices Low

  • Global sourcing & manufacturing scale
  • Flat-pack design slashes transport costs
  • Efficient, automated production lines
  • In-store layout reduces sales staff needed
  • Customer self-assembly as cost lever
02 · Differentiation

What Makes IKEA Stand Out

  • Functional, stylish Scandinavian design
  • Unique, destination in-store experience
  • Sustainability & responsible sourcing
  • Strong emotional brand identity & trust
  • Solutions for everyday life, not just objects
03 · Hybrid Strategy

Avoiding the "Middle Trap"

  • Clear value prop: good design, low price
  • Operational excellence enables both goals
  • Continuous material & design innovation
  • Customer involvement reinforces identity
04 · Business Model Evolution

Towards a Circular Value Chain

  • IKEA Preowned — buy-back & resale
  • Take-back & recycling programmes
  • Sustainable materials & packaging
  • Repair, refurbish & remanufacture

Why This Case Study Matters

The flat-pack isn't just cost-cutting — it is the brand. Carrying your BILLY bookcase to the car is part of the IKEA story. The cost savings and the customer experience are the same decision. That's genius hybrid strategy: your cost lever doubles as your differentiator.

Now IKEA is extending this logic further. Circularity isn't greenwashing — it's a strategic move. Furniture-as-a-service extends customer lifetime value, reduces material input costs, and creates a new revenue stream. IKEA is once again refusing to choose between doing good and doing well.

Final Thoughts

What All Three Cases Have in Common

After working through these three cases, the common thread is clear: sustained competitive advantage isn't a clever trick — it's a system that takes years to build and rivals can't easily copy.

Canal+ has geography and content depth. Amazon has its tech ecosystem and data flywheel. Walmart has physical scale. IKEA has an integrated cost-plus-design model baked into every part of the business. In every case, competitors can see the advantage — the barrier is that copying it would take a decade and billions of dollars.

The frameworks (SWOT, VRIO, business-level strategy) aren't just academic boxes to tick. They're lenses that force you to ask: what's actually hard to replicate here? And if the honest answer is "not much," that's the most important strategic insight of all.
