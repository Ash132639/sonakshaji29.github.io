Strategic Analysis in Action

From internal strengths to competitive advantage — lessons from Canal+, Amazon, Walmart & IKEA

Strategic Frameworks  ·  Tools & Analysis  ·  Real-World Strategy

Understanding how companies win is not about luck — it's about identifying the right resources, leveraging genuine strengths, and building strategies that competitors simply cannot copy.

This week's seminar walked through three powerful frameworks — SWOT, VRIO, and Business-Level Strategy — applied to real companies: Canal+, Amazon vs Walmart, and IKEA. Here's a breakdown of the key insights.

Seminar Task 1

Canal+: Reading the Strategic Chessboard

Canal+ is one of Europe's most recognisable pay-TV and streaming groups, with deep roots in France and a growing presence across Africa. But in an era defined by Netflix and Disney+, how strong is its strategic position really? A SWOT analysis reveals a nuanced picture.

The Full SWOT Breakdown

Strengths

  • Strong brand and market reputation
  • Premium content & exclusive rights
  • Diverse portfolio — TV, streaming, cinema, production
  • Strong presence in France & Africa
  • Integrated operations

Weaknesses

  • High content acquisition costs
  • Dependence on traditional markets
  • Complex cost structure
  • Competition from global streamers

Opportunities

  • Growth in streaming & digital services
  • Expansion in emerging markets
  • Partnerships & co-productions
  • Personalisation & data analytics
  • Growth in sports broadcasting

Threats

  • Intense competition (Netflix, Disney+, Amazon Prime)
  • Piracy and shifting consumer behaviour
  • Economic downturns
  • Regulatory & content licensing risks

What the SWOT Actually Tells Us

Canal+'s genuine competitive moat lies in its content rights and African market position — two areas where global streamers are still catching up. Its diversified production arm (including StudioCanal) means it can create, not just distribute, premium content.

However, the business sits uncomfortably between two worlds. It's too traditional to compete purely on streaming economics, but too expensive to be a regional niche player. The strategic imperative is clear: accelerate digital transformation while doubling down on its irreplaceable local identity and content advantage.

Reflection Questions to Consider

  1. How well is Canal+ actually leveraging its brand strength in digital channels?
  2. Is its Africa expansion a genuine strategic pillar, or still an afterthought?
  3. Can personalisation and data analytics become a true differentiator, or is it table stakes?
  4. How should it respond to the bundling strategies of tech giants?
Seminar Task 2

Amazon vs. Walmart: The VRIO Battle

Two of the world's largest retailers. One born from a bookshop in a garage. The other from a discount store in Arkansas. Decades on, they're locked in the most consequential retail rivalry in history. The VRIO framework helps us understand why neither has simply crushed the other — and why both still have legitimate claims to competitive advantage.

Resources & Capabilities on the Table

Before applying VRIO, it's worth mapping the key resources both companies bring to the fight:

Resource / Capability Threshold or Distinctive? Who leads?
Technology & Innovation Distinctive Amazon (AWS, AI, automation)
Logistics & Supply Chain Distinctive Both — different models
Brand & Customer Loyalty Distinctive Amazon Prime vs. Walmart+
Data & Analytics Distinctive Amazon (data-driven personalisation)
Retail Operations Threshold Table stakes for both
Scale & Financial Resources Distinctive Both — different leverage
Human Capital Threshold Contested

Applying the VRIO Framework

V
Value

Does the resource create real customer value? Amazon's advanced tech and Walmart's everyday low prices both clearly do — but through opposite mechanisms.

R
Rarity

Is it rare among competitors? Amazon's AWS infrastructure and Walmart's physical store network at scale are genuinely rare assets — neither can be quickly replicated.

I
Imitability

Can rivals copy it cheaply? Amazon's algorithmic personalisation and Walmart's omnichannel integration took decades and billions to build. The barriers are enormous.

O
Organisation

Is the firm structured to exploit it? Both are — but Amazon's flat structure and two-pizza team model gives it an edge in speed of innovation.

The Strategic Takeaway

Amazon wins on technology, personalisation, and third-party ecosystem. Walmart wins on physical reach, supply chain muscle, and price competitiveness at massive scale. Crucially, their distinctive capabilities are hard to swap — Amazon can't easily build 4,700 physical stores; Walmart can't easily become a cloud computing powerhouse.

The real competitive battleground is omnichannel integration: whoever closes the gap between digital and physical first gains a near-permanent advantage. The race is still live.

Business-Level Strategy

IKEA: Refusing to Choose Between Cheap and Good

Most strategy textbooks will tell you to pick a lane: either compete on cost, or compete on differentiation. IKEA's genius — and the reason it has survived and grown for over 80 years — is its disciplined refusal to do either in isolation.

Four Strategic Pillars

01 · Cost Leadership

How IKEA Achieves Low Cost

  • Global sourcing & scale
  • Flat-pack design cuts transport costs
  • Efficient, automated production
  • Optimised logistics & distribution
  • In-store experience reduces labour
02 · Differentiation

What Makes IKEA Unique

  • Functional, stylish & affordable design
  • Unique in-store experience
  • Sustainability & responsible sourcing
  • Strong brand identity & trust
  • Solutions for better everyday life
03 · Hybrid Strategy

Avoiding the "Middle Trap"

  • Clear value prop: good design, low price
  • Operational excellence supports both goals
  • Continuous innovation in design & materials
  • Strong customer focus throughout
04 · Business Model Evolution

Towards a Circular Value Chain

  • IKEA Preowned — product resale
  • Take-back & recycling programmes
  • Sustainable materials & packaging
  • Repair, refurbish & remanufacture

The IKEA Value Chain

✏️Design & Development
🌿Sustainable Sourcing
🏭Efficient Production
🚚Smart Logistics
🛒Store & Omnichannel
🔄Reuse, Repair & Recycle

The most important thing for me is for furniture as a service to make it profitable. We need to scale it up at a higher speed — we need to make it part of the business model.

— CEO Jesper Brodin (Whittington et al., 2023, p. 415)

Why IKEA Is a Strategy Case Study Worth Studying

The reason IKEA keeps appearing in strategy courses is that it genuinely does something most companies fail at: it integrates cost leadership and differentiation in a way that reinforces rather than undermines each. The flat-pack isn't just cost-saving — it's part of the brand's democratic design philosophy. The stores aren't just showrooms — they're entertainment and inspiration that reduces the need for expensive sales staff.

Now, the company is extending this logic to circularity. Furniture-as-a-service, take-back schemes, and repair programmes aren't just good PR — they're a strategic move to extend customer lifetime value and reduce material input costs simultaneously. IKEA is once again refusing to choose between doing good and doing well.

Key Takeaways

What These Three Cases Have in Common

Across Canal+, Amazon vs Walmart, and IKEA, one thread runs consistently: sustained competitive advantage comes from resources and capabilities that are difficult to replicate, not from any single clever move.

Canal+ has geography and content depth. Amazon has its tech ecosystem and data flywheel. Walmart has physical scale and supply chain efficiency. IKEA has an integrated cost-plus-design model built over decades. In each case, the advantage isn't a secret — competitors can see it clearly. The barrier is that copying it would take years, billions, and a complete cultural rewiring.

That's the real lesson of strategic analysis: it's not about finding a clever trick. It's about building something that takes so long to replicate, competitors give up trying.
